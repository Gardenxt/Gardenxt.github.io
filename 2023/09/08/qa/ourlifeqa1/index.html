Hello everybody! Welcome to the July 2023 Patreon Q&A for GB Patch Games. I have made it back from my vacation, and I did get my luggage – for the people who saw that in my update post – and everything is back to normal and just like normal, I’m recording the answers to all the questions everybody sent in. We got a lot of very fun ones this time, so I’ll just get started on replying.

When scripting, what programs do you like to use? And have you ever shared what one of your finished scripts looked like or a scene from a finished one? Thanks so much. I hope you have a great vacation.

Thank you. So far. I’ve always written in Word doc. There was one time I did a little bit in Google Docs, but that was when I was just beginning and it didn’t really work for me. So all of my actual GB Patch games projects have been just written in a Word document. And I did share a small snippet of what a script looks like on Tumblr. I might have done that a couple times, but I can share that here on the Patreon if people would want to see what just the plain text looks like.

如果Cove和MC是最好的朋友，他会如何看待MC要求他成为他们孩子的教父？

What inspired you to create such awesome dating sims? By the way, love your work.

Thank you very much. So I think you mean what inspired me to want to make games rather than what games sort of had an influence on the games I make. What originally encouraged me to want to try making a game is that I was really into Harvest Moon, which is now called Story of Seasons in a lot of English speaking places. I was on a forum and I had my Harvest Moon friends, and I came up with these Harvest Moon OCs, this sort of whole fake Harvest Moon game concept. And I knew I could never make an actual farming game. That was way too intimidating. But I happened to be getting into visual novels at the time, and then I was like, “Maybe I could make a visual novel.” And then, you know, me and my friends could play a little story where you actually talk to and interact with these silly characters I had come up with. And so that’s what I did. And that was the first game I ever made. And I just became very fixated on making visual novels and have never stopped since then. And it’s been ten years.

When you started Our Life, did you expect it to grow and be supported as much as it did?

No! Not at all. I had really wanted to do Our Life, but at that time I was at a point where I was thinking I was going to have to give up on making visual novels. Maybe not games entirely, but at least move into like a different type of game that might have a bigger audience because I didn’t think I would ever get as much support as I had with visual novels. And so it was very surprising, but also a big relief that Our Life got as much support as it did. And I’ve been able to very confidently continue making even more visual novels.

Where did you get the idea to create Cove? I mean, what inspired you to make his appearance and personality?

Well for his appearance, it’s sort of a running joke that I like green hair. And when I was going to make this beach themed game, I knew immediately that the lead would have seafoam green hair. And as a kid, I wanted him to look like a beach boy, but definitely more dorky than cool. And as he grew up, he got a little bit more beachy and a little bit less dorky. And I just tried to pick things that I felt fit the overall aesthetic that I was going for with this game. Personality wise, a lot of it was originally based on the gameplay concepts I came up with the sporty studio system and the warm cold system. Then – like he wasn’t a very interesting character at that point, but it was sort of really trying to get into what he was going through in the actual backstory I’d given him, with his parents being divorced and him moving to this new place, that I was sort of able to finally flesh him out and make him more of a well-rounded person. And then he grew along with the game itself. As the Steps came, I sort of understood what he should be like at that age, rather than knowing for sure ahead of time what he would be like when he was older.

The next comment has multiple questions. The first one is:

What would you say to someone who wants to make a visual novel/dating sim but doesn’t know where to start?

I personally would always recommend that someone who wants to make visual novels at least try to learn how to program it themselves, either by learning ren’py or by choosing a visual novel engine that makes sort of the implementation of a script a lot easier. You’ll just have a much higher chance of finishing a visual novel if you do get comfortable putting it together yourself. And I would say that it’s definitely doable. There are engines that are less intimidating than ones that have real programming involved. And then after that I would say, just try to make your own little visual novel. It could just be like a script where somebody goes to the store and they choose what to buy for dinner that night or whatever, and you just you can just use free to use assets and put it all together and then make a functional build. And then you can go from there! If you know you’re able to do that, you can do your full idea as long as you are willing to put in the time. And if your plans are to do something more complicated than a very basic visual novel with right/wrong choices, I’d still recommend starting with the very straightforward right/wrong choices, and then learning from there. And you can always try to find help on forums or Reddit’s or Discords, and you could look for game jams to join with other people who want to make a game and are likely just starting out. And it’s just good to try and gain experience where you can, and figure out if you even enjoy the process of creating a visual novel.

The third question is:

How has the visual novel making process changed for you since you first started? Do you think you started in the best way or do you wish you could go back and change something? Sorry if these have been asked before.

And my process of actually making a game hasn’t changed that much. Sort of. So, for Project W, it has noticeably changed, but obviously that’s not even technically in development right now. But for all the games I’ve made from the beginning to Our Life: Now and Forever, it’s actually been a pretty similar process. Sometimes I outline more or less, but I’m usually the type of person who I have a general idea of what I want to do, and then I start writing the part that I’m sure of, even if I don’t know other events or other root branches. I just start going for it already. And I’ve almost always written in Word, and that’s still what I’m doing, and I just have a bunch of different word docs for my projects. And that’s remained the same. I even still use the same font type and size that I ended up using in the very first project, which, funny story is, I was working with someone else and they changed the font from the default Times New Roman. And I saw that and I was like, “Oh, I’m going to use that too.” And I’ve used it ever since. And the font I use is Verdana, if you were wondering. However, when it comes to how I was as sort of the person commissioning artists and essentially a project leader, and as you know, the way I budgeted things, I’ve definitely learned a lot. And there are things I wish I could go back and do differently, but, you know, can’t really be helped. When you’re new to this sort of thing you’re going to make mistakes, and you’re not going to make the best choices, or be perfect in how you do things. That’s another reason why I suggest starting off with small projects and game jam projects, because some things you can only learn by going through them and actually trying to work together with other people. And unless you’re intending to be a completely solo dev forever, it’s good to learn how to work with people in a situation with lower stakes than a project you’re hoping is going to make you money.

If this has been asked before, please feel free to ignore this question. Is there a reason you have leaned into the more slice of life theme with these two games and steered away from things like fantasy or mystery? A lot of games like this tend to lean into those themes.

That’s because what I wanted to do with Our Life would have been harder with a mystery or a fantasy or any kind of other genre element. Because what I really wanted to do was give people the feeling as if they were growing up in this place and sort of a sense of nostalgia and to evoke a feeling of sentimentality, and those kinds of emotions. And while you can do that with more non-slice-of-life aspects, it definitely adds a whole new layer of complexity. And I really just wanted to focus on those very real world feelings by having this real world kind of setting, even though there is, you know, the magic, multicolored hair. So it’s not magic for real in this world. People can just somehow naturally be born with it.
